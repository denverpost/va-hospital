NOVEMBER 1995

Department of Veterans Affairs has a chance to take over the closing Fitzsimons Army Medical Center. The department's undersecretary for health, however, recommends against paying an estimated $30 million for moving and renovation costs.

1999

University of Colorado discusses possibility of a shared hospital with VA at Fitzsimons site.

AUGUST 2001

VA Secretary Anthony Principi endorses the idea of a shared hospital.

MARCH 2002

VA consultant report recommends a shared hospital.

MAY 2004

Principi announces the VA will build a medical center on the Fitzsimons campus with "some shared facilities" with the university.

JUNE 2004

VA estimates project cost at $328 million.

DECEMBER 2004

VA inspector general finds deplorable conditions at Denver hospital, but notes previously announced plan to move.

Principi kills the shared hospital plan after veterans object to it. He says the university could not offer the 38 acres VA needs.

JULY 2005

VA settles on separate 31-acre site at Fitzsimons for a 1.1 million square foot hospital. Construction envisioned in 2008.

JANUARY 2006

VA contracts with architect and engineering team, but work is suspended as project plans change.

MAY 2006

House Appropriations Committee votes to cut off funding, noting that the proposed hospital has nearly doubled in price, to $621 million, in two years. The Senate, however includes money for the project in its budget.

APRIL 2007

VA Secretary Jim Nicholson announces state-of-the-art showcase medical center with 1.4 million square feet of building space. Price is pegged at $523 million.

NOVEMBER 2007

Design team goes back to work.

JULY 2008

New VA secretary James Peake reverses course, reviving the idea of a shared hospital and warning Congress that the cost estimate has reached $1 billion. Angry veterans rally at the Colorado Capitol.

JANUARY 2009

Colorado congressional delegation urges Gen. Eric Shinseki, President Obama's nominee for VA secretary, to return to a stand-alone hospital despite rising cost estimates.

MARCH 2009

Shinseki announces an 800,000-square-foot stand-alone hospital campus, killing the shared hospital plan.

APRIL 2009

Early estimate for hospital's main concourse is $13 million.

AUGUST 2009

Shinseki joins veterans and elected officials for a ceremonial groundbreaking at the site.

AUGUST 2010

VA chooses Kiewit-Turner to provide pre-construction services. The department tries an unfamiliar contracting method that calls for the builder to join the architect early in the design phase.

APRIL 2011

Kiewit-Turner alleges design is already $76 million over budget.

SEPTEMBER 2011

Confidential Army Corps of Engineers report warns that VA is not using the new contracting method correctly because much of the design work was done before the contractor was hired.

NOVEMBER 2011

An impasse is broken by a 70-word agreement that obliges VA to provide a design within a $604 million construction budget.

DECEMBER 2011

Construction begins on a 13-building medical campus.

JANUARY 2012

Kiewit sends letter to VA saying new design specifications have a "significant cost impact" on the project.

AUGUST 2012

Final design documents arrive. But a VA consultant estimates they are only 80 percent complete, and a VA engineer says they are missing critical elements.

JANUARY 2013

The consultant, Jacobs Engineering, estimates the project is already $200 million over budget. VA does not inform contractor but sends a letter to design team directing them to get the construction costs back to $604 million.

MARCH 2013

Kiewit-Turner offers a new firm price of $897 million. The VA rejects it.

U.S. Government Accountability Office reports that all four of VA’s major medical centers are hundreds of millions of dollars over initial estimates to Congress, with Aurora project leading the way. VA officials tell investigators there have been no cost overruns since construction began.

JULY 2013

Kiewit-Turner sues the VA, alleging that the government breached the contract. It estimates the medical center will cost more than $1 billion.

AUGUST 2013

Rep. Mike Coffman, the Aurora district’s congressman, asks for a detailed explanation of project cost overruns. The VA inspector general rejects the request, citing pending litigation.

FEBRUARY 2014

Colorado delegation meets with VA chief of staff Jose Riojas, construction chief Glenn Haggstrom and Phillipa Anderson, the lead lawyer, to urge a mediated settlement. Rep. Ed Perlmutter says he warned VA officials they would lose the case.

DECEMBER 2014

Civilian Board of Contract Appeals hands VA a crushing defeat, ruling that the agency drove up the cost of the project. Construction shuts down, but restarts with money from Congress and an interim contract.

MARCH 2015

A Corps of Engineers review of the project brings the cost estimate to a stunning new total: $1.73 billion. Haggstrom retires with full benefits.

MAY 2015

Construction nearly shuts down a second time until a last-minute deal in Congress keeps the project alive for a few more months. Veterans rally to "finish the damn thing."

JUNE 2015

VA scales back cost estimate to $1.675 billion by delaying a nursing home and post-traumatic stress center. That reduces hospital beds from 182 to 152. Phillipa Anderson retires with full benefits.

JULY 2015

VA issues new building-by-building cost estimates. The buildings, including parking garages, have doubled to tripled in cost. The arched main concourse, which one Kiewit executive called possibly the most humongous hospital lobby in the world, is expected to cost $120 million.
